Prospects for RBI policy meeting: May further increase inflation tolerance

The RBI will hold its first bimonthly monetary policy meeting of the fiscal year on April 6-8, and will announce a new interest rate policy and monetary policy stance on the 8th. Although India’s domestic inflationary pressure continues to rise, considering the apparent weakening of India’s economic growth momentum, the RBI may further increase its inflation tolerance, and the policy focus will still be on promoting economic growth and will not be disturbed by inflation.

One of the backgrounds of this RBI meeting is the continued rise in domestic inflation in India. In the first two months of this year, India’s domestic consumer price index (CPI) has continuously exceeded the 6% medium-term inflation target set by the Reserve Bank of India, reaching 6.01% and 6.07% respectively. The data for March has not been released, but considering the sharp rise in domestic fuel prices and edible oil prices in India, India’s CPI in March may still be above 6%.

A survey report previously released by the Federation of Indian Chambers of Commerce and Industry (FICCI) showed that India’s CPI-based inflation rate in the fourth quarter of the 2021/2022 fiscal year (i.e. January to March 2022) was 6.0%, and the 2022/23 fiscal year’s inflation rate was 6.0%. The first quarter (i.e. April-June 2022) was 5.5%. This greatly exceeded the previous expectations of the RBI. At its bi-monthly monetary policy meeting in February, the RBI expected India’s inflation to peak in January and then fall month by month, averaging around 5.7 percent in January-March this year.

As early as at the bi-monthly monetary policy meeting in December last year, RBI Governor Das pointed out that the central bank needs to be vigilant about inflation in India and closely monitor the development of inflation. Dharmakirti Joshi, chief economist at Crisil, an Indian economic research institute, pointed out that if international crude oil prices average $85-90 a barrel, India’s average inflation rate will remain at 5.4% in the 2022/23 fiscal year. If the current geopolitical conflict persists, Indian inflation trends will move firmly upwards.

But this does not mean that the RBI will raise interest rates at this week’s monetary policy meeting.

First of all, India’s inflation is more of an imported inflation, and the central bank’s interest rate hike will not have an obvious effect on stabilizing inflation. Since 80% of India’s crude oil and 95% of rapeseed oil depend on the international market, the rise in the prices of these commodities in the international market will soon be transmitted to India, causing domestic inflation in India. Since March 22, retail gasoline and diesel prices in India have continued to rise.

Michael Debabrata Patra, a member of the RBI’s Monetary Policy Committee, pointed out, “Inflation surge in India is not driven by excess demand, but by lack of supply. The role of monetary policy is to align demand with supply. When inflation is driven by demand , monetary policy can stabilize inflation and growth. Monetary policy cannot stabilize when inflation is driven by insufficient supply. The RBI will be faced with a choice: either accept higher inflation for a period of time, or prepare for demand destruction Be responsible for.”

At the same time, India’s economic growth momentum has weakened significantly. The latest data showed that India’s manufacturing expansion has slowed. Data released on the 4th showed that India’s manufacturing purchasing managers’ index (PMI) slowed to 54.0 in March from 54.9 in the previous month. IHS Markit India economist De Lima pointed out that in March, Indian manufacturing companies’ overseas orders decreasing. Due to rising raw material prices, Indian manufacturing input costs are increasing, and companies have to raise selling prices to maintain profits. At the same time, Indian manufacturing entrepreneurs are very worried about upward price pressures. “For now, demand in India is strong enough to withstand price increases, but if inflation continues to pick up pace, we may see a more significant slowdown in manufacturing.” Lima said.

In addition, external uncertainties, including geopolitical tension, tightening of U.S. monetary policy, and repeated world economic growth, will have a significant spillover impact on India’s economic growth. , the RBI noted that the rapid spread of Omikron and related restrictions have dampened global economic activity since its bimonthly monetary policy meeting in December. The global composite purchasing managers’ index (PMI) fell to an 18-month low of 51.4 in January, with weakness in both services and manufacturing.

This also makes India’s economic growth prospects cloudy. Several institutions have already lowered their forecasts for India’s economic growth. Indian rating agency ICRA has downgraded India’s GDP growth forecast for fiscal 2022/23 to 7.2% from 8% previously.

But raising interest rates will not ease inflation, it will hit already slowing economic growth: In this case, the RBI may have to raise the inflation tolerance ceiling and focus policy on boosting economic growth. On March 21, RBI Governor Das said that the RBI can handle the adverse factors facing economic growth well and will continue to stabilize and promote economic growth with liquidity. This may be seen as the current basic attitude of the RBI.
