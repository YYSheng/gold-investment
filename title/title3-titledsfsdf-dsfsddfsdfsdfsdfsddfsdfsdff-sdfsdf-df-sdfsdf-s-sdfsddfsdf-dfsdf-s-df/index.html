The minutes of the Fed meeting again signaled a 50 basis point rate hike to support a monthly reduction of $95 billion in the balance sheet

The minutes of the Fed meeting released on Wednesday (April 6) local time showed that Fed officials discussed how to reduce the balance sheet plan at the March meeting. may be appropriate, and indicated that one or more rate hikes of 50 basis points may be required.

Federal Reserve officials generally support the monthly reduction of the upper limit of treasury assets by 60 billion US dollars and the mortgage-backed securities (MBS) of 35 billion US dollars.

The minutes showed that participants agreed that shrinking the balance sheet would play an important role in firming the stance of monetary policy and expected it would be appropriate to start the process at an upcoming meeting, possibly as early as May.

In addition to discussions about shrinking balance sheets, officials discussed a roadmap for future rate hikes, and they favored more aggressive moves. The minutes mentioned that many Fed members would likely have called for a 50 basis point rate hike in March, had it not been for the Ukraine conflict.

It also means that the probability of raising interest rates by 50 basis points at the upcoming meeting has greatly increased, which is also in line with the market’s current expectations. In recent days, Fed officials have also frequently released hawkish signals, paving the way for the next rate hike of 50 basis points.

“Many participants pointed out that one or more rate hikes of 50 basis points may be needed at future meetings, especially if inflationary pressures continue to rise or intensify,” the minutes of the meeting showed.

Quincy Krosby, chief equity strategist at LPL Financial, said the minutes were a warning to anyone who thinks the Fed will take a more dovish tack on inflation. Their message is: You are wrong.

Chris Zaccarelli, chief investment analyst at Independent Advisors Alliance, said the $95 billion-a-month cut is a “good start” but the Fed may need to cut spending at a faster pace to fight inflation. At the current rate, it may take more than 5 years (and possibly as long as 8 years) to fully realize all securities held. Before that happens, we have a good chance of a recession, which could force the Fed to increase its balance sheet again, so market distortions could persist for a long time.
