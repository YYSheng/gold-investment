The EU’s oil embargo on Russia is still uncertain, Brent oil closed up close to $100

On April 7, Brent oil rose 0.34% in late trading to close at $101.41 per barrel. Oil prices fell to as low as $98.41 at one point in the session after the consumer countries announced a massive release of oil from emergency reserves. But the European Union continued to debate how to deal with the Russian oil problem, supporting oil prices to end higher in late trade

On Thursday (April 7), it rose 0.16% to close at $97.16 per barrel, after falling to $93.81 during the session, the lowest since March 16. Earlier, the International Energy Agency (IEA) announced the details of the 120 million barrels of oil release plan. The United Kingdom will release 4.41 million barrels of crude oil reserves; Germany will release 6.48 million barrels of crude oil reserves; France will release 6.05 million barrels of crude oil reserves, causing oil prices to drop to nearly 3. Weekly low.

The European Union agreed to ban coal imports from Russia, according to people familiar with the matter, in its first action against Moscow’s key energy revenue. EU diplomats on Thursday signed off on the sanctions package, which also includes a ban on most Russian trucks and ships from entering the bloc, which has been coordinated with the United States and Britain. Previously, the EU did not impose sanctions on Russia’s energy sector due to Germany and Hungary’s reliance on Russian fossil fuels.

Some member states continue to push for further measures, notably on Russian energy imports and oil, and will discuss ideas such as creating escrow accounts to freeze additional profits from higher oil prices since the conflict began. Josep Borrell, vice-president of the European Commission and high representative for foreign affairs and security policy, told a NATO meeting that the next step would be to discuss an oil embargo.

Bob Yawger, head of energy futures at Mizuho, ​​said: “No one is willing to take the pain of sanctioning the Russian energy sector, which is supporting the market.” John Kilduff, partner at Again Capital LLC, said: “The demand situation in Asia’s big oil consumers is really looking bad. Well, especially with so much new supply on the market.”

Meanwhile, the U.S. House of Representatives passed a bill banning U.S. imports of oil, gas and coal from Russia and sent it to President Biden for signature. The House voted 413-9 to pass the bill; earlier Thursday, the Senate passed it unanimously by a 100-0 vote. Once Biden signs it, the order he issued last month will become law. Congress also passed a bill on Thursday that would remove Russia and Belarus from normal trade relations, allowing the United States to impose steep tariffs on goods from Russia and Belarus. Including crude oil and all other petroleum products, Russia will account for about 8 percent of U.S. oil imports in 2021, according to the U.S. Energy Information Administration.

Russian Deputy Prime Minister Alexander Novak said Russia’s April oil production may fall by 4%-5% from March due to insurance and vessel usage issues. Global oil demand is rising, and oil prices reflect the current situation. There are no major problems in the oil market. The International Energy Agency expects Russia’s oil production to fall by a quarter this month, leaving global energy supplies short of a massive 3 million barrels a day. Draining emergency reserves will fill the gap to some extent. Only 568.3 million barrels of U.S. crude oil reserves remain, the lowest level since May 2002, IEA data showed. If 180 million barrels of crude are released as planned, the U.S. strategic stockpile would fall to its lowest level since 1984.
