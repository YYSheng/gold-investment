Spot gold is lower, FED may surprise next month, Russia-Ukraine conflict in disguise is good for the dollar

Spot gold traded lower on Wednesday (April 6) as the U.S. dollar index hit a new high of 99.759 since May 6, 2020, following more hawkish remarks from Fed officials. The prospect of new Western sanctions on Russia would be negative for the euro, which in turn would be positive for the dollar.

The minutes of the March Fed meeting will be released at 2:00 Beijing time on Thursday (April 7) and may provide clues about how quickly policymakers want to shrink their balance sheets and raise interest rates. The Fed’s stance has become increasingly hawkish recently.

Brainard: The balance sheet will be reduced as early as May

Fed Governor Brainard said on Tuesday (April 5) that she expects methodical interest rate hikes and a rapid shrinking of the Fed’s balance sheet to shift monetary policy to a “more neutral stance” later this year, adding that it will move as needed Further tighten the policy.

“I think we all absolutely agree that inflation is too high, and lowering it is the most important thing,” Brainard said at a Minneapolis Fed meeting, adding that the Fed will begin rapidly ramping up its response to nearly 90 percent of inflation as early as next month. The pace of shrinking the trillion-dollar balance sheet will be “much faster” than the last time it was shrunk.

This is the first time Brainard has spoken publicly since the Fed started a new round of rate hikes last month. The hawkish remarks by Brainard, who is usually seen as leaning toward the dovish side, have investors worried about “the speed and strength of the Fed’s balance sheet reduction.”

San Francisco Fed President Daly said on Tuesday that interest rate hikes aimed at reducing excessive inflation will not tip the U.S. economy into recession. “It’s possible that the economy may slow down and it may look like it’s close to a recession, but I expect it to be short-lived and then the economy will bounce back.”

The market believes that the Fed will act faster, and announced a 50 basis point interest rate hike in both May and June, and the interest rate is expected to reach 2.5%-2.75% by the end of the year. That would be above the “neutral” level of 2.4 percent considered by most policymakers.

Hawkish comments from key Fed members are likely to overshadow the upcoming Fed minutes as they point to a 50bps rate hike at the next meeting and a faster-than-expected balance sheet shrinkage, which could give gold a boost New selling pressure.

West prepares new sanctions against Russia

EUR/USD hit a new low of 1.0873 since March 8. The United States and its allies on Wednesday prepared to impose new sanctions on Moscow over the killing of civilians in northern Ukraine, which Ukrainian President Volodymyr Zelensky called an “outrage of war” and demanded appropriate punishment. Russia again shelled Mariupol and Kharkiv in Ukraine on Wednesday.

Proposed EU sanctions would ban the import of Russian coal and ban Russian ships from EU ports. Germany’s foreign minister said the coal ban was the first step towards banning all Russian fossil fuel imports, signaling a growing EU resolve.

About a third of Europe’s natural gas comes from Russia, and Europe has been worried about the economic impact of a complete ban on Russian energy supplies. German Finance Minister Lindner told the Die Zeit weekly newspaper that he wants Germany to stop importing Russian oil and gas as soon as possible, but that is not feasible at the moment.

European Commission President von der Leyen said they were also discussing a ban on oil imports. Ukraine said the embargo on Russian gas was crucial to securing a deal in peace talks to end the war.

The world is facing an era of high inflation as relations between the West and Russia deteriorate and globalization reverses in the wake of the COVID-19 pandemic. Experts predict inflation will exceed 4.5% in most of Europe over the next two years.

Ray Attrill, global head of FX strategy at National Australia Bank, said: “Brainard’s hawkish rhetoric is the immediate cause of the dollar’s strength, but given the euro’s weight in the dollar index, inflation needs to be factored into the euro when we talk about the dollar, and recently Talks about expanding sanctions are not doing the euro any favors.”
