Oil prices tumbled 13% this week, a record in nearly two years as IEA members agree to release oil reserves

On Friday (April 1), Brent oil futures fell 0.3% to settle at $104.39 a barrel. The U.S. and its allies agreed to a second coordinated release of oil reserves in a month, the International Energy Agency (IEA) said, but the decline in oil prices was limited by the lack of specifics on how much to release.

The IEA issued a statement after Friday’s emergency meeting, saying the statement showed the “strong and unified commitment” of IEA member countries to stabilizing global energy markets. Details of the new emergency reserve release will be announced early next week. Hidechika Koizumi, head of Japan’s Ministry of Economy, Trade and Industry’s international affairs department, told reporters: “Given the current situation… the participants at the IEA meeting agreed on the additional release itself, but they were not able to agree on the total amount and the quota for each country. On March 1, the International Energy Agency convened the largest release of oil reserves in history, releasing nearly 62 million barrels of oil, about half of which came from the United States.

To fill the gap created by sanctions and buyers shunning Russian oil, U.S. President Joe Biden on Thursday approved the release of 1 million barrels a day, equivalent to 1.8 million barrels a day, from the U.S. Strategic Petroleum Reserve for six months starting in May billion barrels. This is the largest release of the Strategic Petroleum Reserve (SPR) in U.S. history. Biden said U.S. allies and partners could release an additional 30 million to 50 million barrels of oil. “The imminent release of reserves in the U.S. does not change the fact that the market will struggle to find enough supply in the coming months,” said PVM analyst Stephen Brennock.

The two major crude oil indicators are currently steady near 100 yuan, underscoring supply concerns, as the release of oil reserves is difficult to make up for the 3 million barrels per day of Russian oil lost in the global market, according to IEA estimates. An analysis of IEA data shows that even before the March 1 release, oil inventories controlled by member governments were at their lowest level since 2005, while the U.S. strategic oil reserve level fell to its lowest level since 2002. .

The Organization of the Petroleum Exporting Countries (OPEC) and its allies including Russia, the OPEC+ alliance, agreed on Thursday to stick with a plan to increase output by a modest 432,000 barrels per day per month, defying pressure to increase output more.

The Organization of the Petroleum Exporting Countries (OPEC) pumped 28.54 million bpd in March, up 90,000 bpd from February but below the 253,000 bpd required by its deal with allies including Russia, according to the survey. . The agreement calls for all OPEC+ members to increase production by 400,000 bpd in March, with about 253,000 bpd being the responsibility of the 10 OPEC producers. Oil supply disruptions in some African member nations partially offset increased output from Saudi Arabia and other major producers.

Production growth from October to January was below the pledged level, only surpassing the pledged increase in February as many producers lacked the capacity to produce more crude due to insufficient investment, a situation exacerbated by the pandemic . As a result, oil production by the 10 OPEC members is well below the level stipulated in the agreement. The survey found that OPEC’s compliance with its output-cut agreement reached 151 percent, compared with 136 percent in February.

The investigation found that Nigeria’s oil production fell by 100,000 bpd due to a force majeure event in the Bonny and Blas river streams. The force majeure on the Brass was lifted. Libya’s oil production fell by 50,000 barrels a day due to the shutdown of two fields earlier this month. These production disruptions offset output growth in OPEC’s major producers. The survey found that Saudi Arabia recorded the biggest increase in output in March, rising by 110,000 barrels per day.

U.S. energy companies increased the number of rigs drilling for a second week in a row last week, but the growth rate remained slow, as drillers continued to cash back their profits from higher oil prices to shareholders rather than boost output.
