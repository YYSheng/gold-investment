Stock market trading hours reduced to 2 hours, or an additional rest day, South Asian countries deal with energy shortages

Since March, the whole country of Sri Lanka has been in a power shortage. The Sri Lanka Public Utilities Commission has announced that the nationwide power outage will be extended to 13 hours a day from March 31.

Affected by the power outage, Sri Lanka’s Colombo Stock Exchange announced on March 31 that it would reduce its daily trading hours to two hours, from 10:30 am to 12:30 noon each day. The Sri Lankan stock market previously traded for 4.5 hours a day.

A similar situation is also happening in Nepal, which is also a South Asian country. Southern Nepal needs to import electricity from India during the dry season. Because of the price increase, Nepal cannot afford it and is forced to cut off electricity in the country. In fact, the shortage of electricity is not only a problem for these countries. India has also faced a shortage of power supply recently, and has taken some measures to curb electricity.

The stock market is only open for two hours a day

The Colombo Stock Exchange said in a statement that the decision was made because the power outage prevented investors and shareholders from trading normally. Affected by this news, after the country’s stock market opened on March 31, the main stock indexes fell more than 5%.

Of course, it is not only the securities market that is affected, Janaka Ratnayake, chairman of the Sri Lanka Public Utilities Commission, told the media on March 30: “We have asked the government to allow about 1.3 million public sector employees to work in the next two years. Work from home during the day, so you can better deal with fuel and power shortages.”

Part of the reason for the massive blackout, Ratnayake said, was the government’s inability to pay $52 million for the 37,000 tonnes of diesel waiting to be unloaded. “We don’t have foreign currency to pay,” Ratnayake said, adding that there will be more blackouts in the next two days, but “that’s the reality.”

However, on March 31, two senior Sri Lankan officials revealed that the country had purchased a batch of coal from Russia at a favorable price, which is expected to arrive in Sri Lanka in the second week of April, which will partially alleviate the current energy shortage. question.

About 40% of Sri Lanka’s electricity comes from hydropower, but due to the combination of scarce rainfall and a dry season, most reservoirs are at low levels of extreme water scarcity. The other 60% of electricity depends on thermal power, but Sri Lanka currently lacks enough foreign exchange to buy imported coal and oil, and many thermal power plants have difficulty producing.

The new crown epidemic has severely disrupted Sri Lanka’s tourism industry, exacerbating its domestic economic problems. Sri Lanka’s foreign exchange reserves have fallen by 70% in the past two years. As of February, foreign exchange reserves were only $2.31 billion, but this year there are $6.9 billion in debt to be repaid.

Sri Lanka has issued an import ban on some commodities in March 2020 to save foreign exchange. Sri Lankan Finance Minister Basil Rajapaksa will visit Washington in April for talks with the International Monetary Fund (IMF). The IMF previously said in an assessment report that Sri Lanka is experiencing a dual crisis of balance of payments and sovereign debt and needs a “comprehensive strategy” to make its debt sustainable.

Add a day off?

Nepal is currently facing a similar energy crisis. According to Nepalese media reports, Nepal Petroleum Corporation, the country’s only oil importer, has written to the Nepalese government, suggesting that Sunday should also be temporarily designated as a public holiday to save consumption and overcome the energy crisis by reducing public transportation and government operations. . Currently, Nepal’s rest day is only Saturday.

During the dry season, Nepal needs to import electricity from India to make up for its own lack of production capacity. Nepal Electricity Authority spokesman Suresh Bahadur Bhattarai said that Nepal is suffering from the knock-on effects of the situation in Ukraine, “in the context of high global energy prices, India’s electricity prices have been rising”, which ultimately makes Nepal unaffordable.

Coal prices have hit record highs since the escalation in Ukraine. Most Indian power companies import coal, which is mixed with local coal to generate electricity, and nearly 70% of India’s electricity comes from coal. High prices have led Indian coal importers to slow the pace of imports.

Coal inventories fell as the Indian government cut coal supplies to non-power sectors, including aluminum smelters and steelmakers, amid fears that high summer temperatures could further exacerbate coal shortages. Last October, India experienced its worst coal shortage in recent years, causing power outages in some states for up to 14 hours, dealing a huge blow to the economic recovery.
